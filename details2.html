The Cuban Missile Crisis of October 1962 was a direct and dangerous confrontation between the United States and the Soviet Union during the Cold War. It was the moment when the two superpowers came closest to nuclear conflict. The crisis was sparked by the discovery of Soviet ballistic missiles deployed in Cuba, which were capable of striking the United States.

For thirteen days, the world held its breath as President John F. Kennedy and Soviet Premier Nikita Khrushchev engaged in intense negotiations. The crisis was resolved when Khrushchev agreed to remove the missiles from Cuba in exchange for a pledge from the United States not to invade the island and a secret agreement to remove U.S. missiles from Turkey.

The Cuban Missile Crisis is often regarded as the moment when the world came closest to nuclear war, and it had a profound impact on international relations, leading to increased efforts to avoid direct conflict between the superpowers.
